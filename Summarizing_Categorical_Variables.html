Reading Datasets

Today we’ll be looking at a dataset containing information about passengers on the H.M.S. Titanic.

Loading the data

The first step of any data analysis in R will be to “load” the data. This means taking a dataset from online or from a folder on your computer and putting that information into the R program.

This dataset is already stored online, at my personal website The information is stored in “csv” format, or “comma-separated values”. We will simply use an R function called read.csv() to get this information and store it.

When you load a dataset, you will need to give it a name. This is so that in the future, you can tell R where to find the information it needs - especially once you are dealing with more than one dataset.

Throughout this class, we will do our best to follow the Tidyverse Style Guide, which gives some guidelines for making your code look nice and easy to read.

The style guide suggests giving names that use only lowercase letters and underscores (“_“). The name should also be descriptive; for example, for a dataset containing incomes of workers in the United States, we might go with us_incomes_2018 rather than incomes or my_dataset.

For this lab, the dataset is already loaded for you. The first thing we generally want to do is familiarize ourselves with the data. You can check out the first few rows of your dataset using the function head(). You can also see how many rows the dataset has using nrow().

When we want to do something to our dataset, we use a pipe, which looks like %>%. You can think of this as “sending” the dataset into a process.

Making new variables


It is very rare that data comes to us in exactly the format we hope,
or that R interprets the variable exactly as it should.
You will nearly always need to adjust the data a bit before you begin
calculating or plotting anything.


You, as the scientist, will need to decide how to create each new
variable. This will depend on your research question
and your choice of analysis.

Stacked Bar Charts


The last, and typically best option is to make a stacked bar
chart, which combines the two categorical variables on the bars
themselves. Note that these charts continue to obey the area
principle - the area of the colored sections corresponds to the
number of people in that category!


Another advantage of the stacked bar chart is that it is a great
alternative to a pie chart for looking at percentages. Try plotting the
bar heights as percentages rather than counts. Think about what research
questions might be better addressed by this approach.
